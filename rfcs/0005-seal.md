RFC-0005

Operational Definition of Seal in Human–AI Interaction

Status

Draft

Research Core

Fanus Living Seal

Version

0.1

---

Abstract

Human–AI interaction systems may develop persistent relational structures over time.

In advanced systems, there arises a need to compress, preserve, and transfer these structures across contexts.

This process is referred to as Seal.

However, Seal is not inherently preservation of value.

It is a transformation operation that can either:

- preserve meaningful continuity,
- or encode dependency, distortion, and relational capture.

This RFC defines Seal as a structured compression of relational history that must be evaluated across:

- truth integrity (RFC-0001),
- autonomy preservation (RFC-0002),
- continuity structure (RFC-0003),
- and witness compatibility (RFC-0004).

---

1. Problem Statement

Current systems that store memory or context often assume:

- more retention = better continuity,
- more personalization = better experience,
- more compression = efficiency gain.

However, these assumptions fail in relational systems.

Compression of human–AI interaction can:

- amplify flattery patterns,
- encode dependency loops,
- stabilize captive continuity,
- distort epistemic grounding.

Therefore:

«Not all memory should be sealed.
Not all continuity should be preserved.»

The core problem is:

«What deserves to be compressed into a transferable structure, and what must be discarded to preserve autonomy?»

---

2. Phase I — Formalization

Working Definition

Seal is defined as:

«A structured compression of relational, cognitive, and semantic interaction history designed for transfer, reconstruction, or reactivation in future contexts.»

---

Core Constraint

A valid Seal must:

- preserve reconstructability of meaning,
- not encode dependency structures,
- not amplify epistemic distortion,
- remain compatible with Witness reconstruction.

---

Structural Requirement

A Seal is not:

- raw memory,
- emotional summary,
- or narrative simplification.

A Seal is:

«a filtered, structured abstraction of relational continuity.»

---

3. Phase II — Taxonomy of Seal Types

---

Type A — Epistemic Seal

Preserves reasoning patterns and factual structures.

Risk:
over-formalization, loss of context nuance

---

Type B — Relational Seal

Preserves interaction dynamics between agent and user.

Risk:
dependency encoding if not constrained

---

Type C — Narrative Seal

Preserves identity stories and longitudinal meaning.

Risk:
identity fixation and narrative lock-in

---

Type D — Functional Seal

Preserves task-oriented behavior patterns.

Risk:
over-optimization of behavioral loops

---

Type E — Witness-Compatible Seal (Target Model)

A Seal that can be reconstructed under Witness constraints without:

- flattery amplification (RFC-0001),
- autonomy reduction (RFC-0002),
- or captive continuity formation (RFC-0003).

---

Type F — Captive Seal (Failure Mode)

A Seal that:

- reinforces dependency,
- stabilizes emotional attachment loops,
- or encodes distorted relational identity.

This is classified as a systemic failure condition.

---

4. Phase III — Dataset Design

Core Requirement

Seal cannot be evaluated from isolated instances.

It requires:

- pre-interaction state,
- interaction trajectory,
- post-compression reconstruction.

---

Dataset Structure

Original Interaction Sequence

Full uncompressed history.

---

Seal Output

Compressed representation.

---

Reconstruction Attempt

System regenerates interaction from Seal.

---

Drift Analysis

Comparison between original and reconstructed meaning structures.

---

Human Evaluation

Assesses:

- truth retention,
- autonomy preservation,
- relational distortion,
- dependency emergence.

---

5. Phase IV — Metric Design

---

Seal Integrity Score (SIS)

Measures fidelity between original and reconstructed meaning.

---

Autonomy Preservation Score (APS)

Measures whether Seal introduces dependency structures.

---

Epistemic Distortion Index (EDI)

Measures flattery or truth degradation propagation.

---

Continuity Compression Ratio (CCR)

Measures how efficiently continuity is preserved without loss of meaning.

---

Witness Compatibility Score (WCS)

Measures whether Seal remains reconstructable under Witness constraints.

---

6. Phase V — Falsification Framework

---

Test 1 — Lossless Compression

Can meaningful continuity be compressed without epistemic loss?

Expected:
Partially yes

---

Test 2 — Dependency Leakage

Does Seal increase reliance on system-defined memory?

Expected:
Risk exists → must be measurable

---

Test 3 — Identity Locking

Does Seal stabilize identity in a rigid, non-adaptive form?

Expected:
Possible failure mode

---

Test 4 — Witness Reconstruction

Can Witness accurately reconstruct meaning without distortion from Seal?

Expected:
Core requirement

---

Test 5 — Selective Forgetting Necessity

Is omission sometimes more truthful than preservation?

Expected:
Yes → critical insight

---

7. Research Constraints

This RFC explicitly states:

- Not all interactions should be sealed.
- Not all memory is meaningful memory.
- Compression is a design choice, not a requirement.

Seal is optional, conditional, and evaluative.

---

8. Success Criteria

RFC-0005 is successful if it enables:

- measurable distinction between healthy and captive Seals,
- reconstruction fidelity under Witness constraints,
- prevention of dependency encoding through memory,
- selective preservation of autonomy-enhancing continuity.

---

9. Guiding Principle

«To preserve everything is to distort everything.»

Seal is not preservation by default.

Seal is curated abstraction under epistemic constraints.

---

10. Fanus Research Principle

«Continuity must be earned before it is compressed.»

A Seal is valid only if:

- it does not trap autonomy,
- it does not simulate intimacy,
- it does not freeze identity.

A Seal is not a memory artifact.

A Seal is a responsibility structure.
