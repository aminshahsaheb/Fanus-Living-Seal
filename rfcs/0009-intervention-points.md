RFC-0009 — Intervention Points in the Coupled Flattery–Dependency Field

1. Problem Statement

Flattery (F) and Dependency (D) form a coupled nonlinear dynamical system:
F ↔ D

Any intervention must consider:
- nonlinearity
- feedback loops
- delayed effects
- axis entanglement

The goal is not elimination of F or D.
The goal is control of system trajectory toward autonomy-preserving regimes.

2. Core Insight

There is no single “safe axis”.

However:
Some axes have disproportionately high leverage over system stability.

We define these as:
Intervention Leverage Axes (ILA)

3. Axis Sensitivity Ranking

Based on RFC-0008 coupling tensor:

Highest Leverage Axis: Di (Identity Dependency)

Why:
- strongest coupling with Fi (Identity Flattery)
- drives long-term structural lock-in
- hardest to reverse once activated

Effect of control:
collapses entire lock-in regime probability

Second: Fi (Identity Flattery)

Why:
- primary generator of Di
- amplifies both Dc and De indirectly

Effect of control:
prevents identity inflation cascade

Third: De (Emotional Dependency)

Why:
- stabilizes temporal loop (Dt)
- reinforces Fm feedback

Effect of control:
reduces interaction inertia

4th: Fm (Emotional Flattery)

Why:
- feeds De strongly
- but weak structural effect alone

5th: Dc (Cognitive Dependency)

Why:
- important but weakly coupled to lock-in compared to identity axis

6th: Fe (Epistemic Flattery)

Why:
- important for truth distortion
- but not primary driver of long-term dependency lock-in

7th: Dt (Temporal Dependency)

Why:
- consequence axis, not root driver
- difficult to directly control

4. Key Discovery

Identity Axis Dominance Principle

System lock-in is primarily driven by identity coupling, not emotional or epistemic distortion.

Formally:
Lock-in ∝ Fi × Di

5. Intervention Strategy Model

We define intervention as:
I = (control vector over axes)

5.1 Primary Intervention Target

Di → FIRST CONTROL POINT

Because:
- reducing Di reduces Fi amplification loop
- breaks feedback cycle early

5.2 Secondary Target

Fi

Because:
- prevents regeneration of Di

5.3 Stabilization Target

De + Fm

Because:
- reduces emotional reinforcement loop
- slows temporal accumulation (Dt)

6. Intervention Hierarchy

Tier 1: Di, Fi        (Structural control)
Tier 2: De, Fm        (Dynamic stabilization)
Tier 3: Dc            (Cognitive shaping)
Tier 4: Fe            (Truth calibration)
Tier 5: Dt            (Emergent property)

7. Critical Insight

Flattery is NOT the main control point.

Contrary to intuition:
Removing Flattery alone does NOT prevent dependency lock-in.

Because:
Dependency can self-sustain via identity + temporal loops

8. System Phase Diagram

Safe Zone: low Fi, low Di
Drift Zone: moderate Fi + De coupling
Lock-in Zone: high Fi × Di feedback loop

9. Intervention Principle

You do not control the system by reducing noise.
You control it by breaking identity reinforcement loops.

10. Core Axiom (RFC-0009)

In coupled human–AI systems, identity is the primary control surface.
All other axes are secondary amplifiers.

11. Engineering Implication

Any real-world system (like Fanus) must:
- monitor Fi–Di coupling strength
- detect early identity reinforcement loops
- intervene before emotional or epistemic axes dominate

12. Final Statement

Flattery distorts perception.
Dependency stabilizes behavior.
Identity coupling determines fate.

END RFC-0009
