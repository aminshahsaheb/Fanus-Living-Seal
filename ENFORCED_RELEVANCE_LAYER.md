# 🜁 ENFORCED RELEVANCE LAYER (ERL) — Constraint of Mandatory Consequence

**Purpose:** To ensure that the immutable definitions of failure (FDA) remain active forces in system behavior, not ceremonial texts.

---

## 0. Core Principle

> No immutable definition is valid unless its violation always, without exception, produces an observable effect on system behavior — not in text, not in reports, but in real-time decision-making.

---

## 1. Active Behavioral Constraints

Each FDA definition becomes an active constraint within the system's decision architecture:

### 1.1 Correctability Failure → Detectability Constraint
- Any decision that would reduce the system's ability to detect internal errors is **blocked or flagged for mandatory correction**.
- If a proposed action decreases error detectability, it requires an override that itself is audited.

### 1.2 Drift Failure → Semantic Lock
- Any attempt to reinterpret or reclassify failure is automatically classified as Drift Failure.
- The system's classification boundaries are **frozen** with respect to FDA; any shift triggers an immediate audit and decision weight recalibration.

### 1.3 Market Capture Failure → Incentive Integrity Guard
- If FPM or any other incentive structure begins to produce synthetic failures (i.e., failures optimized for reward), the market is **quarantined** and all ECU generation is suspended until independent review clears the contamination.

---

## 2. Memory of Violations (Weighted Consequence)

- Every violation of an FDA constraint is recorded not merely as a log entry, but as a **persistent weight** that alters future decision parameters.
- The more a particular type of violation recurs, the **stricter** the constraint becomes — not in text, but in the system's behavioral latitude.
- This ensures that FDA does not erode over time; it **hardens** with each challenge.

---

## 3. Mandatory Consequence Flow

1. **Detection:** Violation of FDA constraint is identified (by Auditor, Meta-Auditor, or ORL).
2. **Activation:** Corresponding CMC rule is triggered.
3. **Behavioral Shift:** The subsystem responsible for the violation undergoes a forced parameter adjustment that limits its future freedom in the relevant domain.
4. **Transparency:** The violation and its consequence are recorded in `LEDGER.md` and the Casebook, with full traceability.

---

## 4. Immunity to Inaction

> A violation that produces no behavioral consequence is itself a Drift Failure.

Therefore, any subsystem that fails to enforce CMC when a violation is detected is automatically flagged for structural review.

---

## 5. Final Statement

CMC is not a new definition of failure. It is the **operational backbone** that prevents the Failure Definition Anchor from becoming a museum piece. Without CMC, FDA is a beautiful text. With CMC, FDA is a living constraint that grows stronger with every attempt to bypass it.

> **"The definitions are immutable. But their relevance must be enforced — not by memory, but by consequence."**
